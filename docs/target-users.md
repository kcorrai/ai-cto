# Target Users

## Primary Personas

---

### Persona 1: The Indie Hacker

**Name:** Alex
**Age:** 28–40
**Role:** Software engineer by day, building a SaaS on nights and weekends
**Revenue Stage:** $0 – $2k MRR

**Background:**
Alex has been building side projects for years. He ships quickly, often alone. He knows how to code but lacks the strategic big-picture perspective that comes with managing teams and scaling systems. He builds things that work but often finds out too late that the architecture won't scale, or that he shipped a product missing 3 key features that would have driven retention.

**Goals:**

- Launch his SaaS and get to ramen profitability
- Avoid wasted effort on the wrong features
- Validate technical decisions without hiring a consultant
- Understand if he is "launch-ready"

**Pain Points:**

- No one to review his architecture decisions
- Does not know what he is missing until users churn
- Afraid to launch because he does not know if his code will hold up
- Wastes time on features that do not matter

**Buying Behavior:**

- Pays for tools that directly accelerate his project
- Price-sensitive, but willing to pay $20–50/month if value is clear
- Discovers tools on Product Hunt, Hacker News, Twitter

**Key Jobs to Be Done:**

- "Tell me if I should launch now or keep building"
- "Tell me the 3 most important things to fix before launch"
- "Show me what features I am missing that would reduce churn"

**Success Moment:**
Alex runs AI CTO on his project for the first time and gets a list of 12 specific, prioritized findings. 4 of them are things he had been ignoring. He fixes 2 of them in a weekend and feels confident launching.

### Persona 2: The Technical Founder

**Name:** Sarah
**Age:** 30–45
**Role:** CTO/technical co-founder of a small startup
**Revenue Stage:** $5k – $100k MRR

**Background:**
Sarah is a strong engineer but has grown into a founder role. She writes code, manages a team of 2–4 engineers, and makes architectural decisions. She knows her codebase has problems but does not have time to do a proper audit. She is about to raise a seed round and needs to know her technical house is in order.

**Goals:**

- Identify technical debt before it causes a crisis
- Prepare for investor technical due diligence
- Make strategic build vs. buy decisions
- Scale the team without the codebase becoming unmanageable

**Pain Points:**

- Too busy shipping to do a proper architectural review
- Worried about technical debt she cannot see clearly
- No structured framework for evaluating roadmap priorities
- Due diligence audits are expensive and slow

**Buying Behavior:**

- Budget from company; values ROI over cost
- Willing to pay $100–300/month if it saves engineering time
- Discovers tools through trusted developer communities and referrals

**Key Jobs to Be Done:**

- "Give me an honest assessment of our technical debt"
- "Are we ready for investor technical due diligence?"
- "What should our engineering team focus on this quarter?"
- "What security risks do we have that we do not know about?"

**Success Moment:**
Sarah runs AI CTO before her seed round pitch. The analysis surfaces 3 security issues and 2 scalability risks. She fixes them in 2 weeks and goes into due diligence with confidence.

### Persona 3: The Solo Developer / Freelancer

**Name:** Marcus
**Age:** 25–38
**Role:** Freelance developer building products for clients and himself
**Revenue Stage:** Variable

**Background:**
Marcus ships code for clients but also launches his own projects. He is a strong tactician but often lacks the strategic lens to evaluate whether his work is positioned for long-term success. He wants to add more value to clients by offering "AI CTO audits" as an additional service.

**Goals:**

- Deliver higher-quality technical recommendations to clients
- Upsell technical auditing as a service
- Build his own products more strategically

**Pain Points:**

- Hard to explain technical risks to non-technical clients
- No structured deliverable format for technical recommendations
- Limited time for deep architectural reviews on every project

**Buying Behavior:**

- Tools that increase billing potential or save time justify higher prices
- Interested in white-label or agency features

**Key Jobs to Be Done:**

- "Generate a polished technical audit report I can send to my client"
- "Help me identify risks in my client's codebase quickly"

---

### Persona 4: The Technical Product Manager

**Name:** Jordan
**Age:** 30–42
**Role:** Senior Product Manager with technical background
**Revenue Stage:** Part of a funded startup ($1M–$20M ARR)

**Background:**
Jordan manages the product roadmap but needs to communicate effectively with engineering. She wants to understand technical debt impact on product velocity, and make data-driven cases for investing in platform work vs. features.

**Goals:**

- Quantify technical debt in business terms (cost to velocity)
- Prioritize technical vs. product work with data
- Identify missing features that competitors have

**Pain Points:**

- Engineering says everything is fine until it breaks
- Hard to get buy-in for platform investment without concrete data
- Cannot objectively evaluate engineering recommendations

**Key Jobs to Be Done:**

- "Show me a business-impact view of our technical debt"
- "What features are we missing compared to our category benchmarks?"
- "Help me build the case for a refactor quarter"

## Secondary Audiences

### Agencies

Agencies serving multiple clients can use AI CTO as a white-labeled audit tool. Team plan features serve this segment.

### Accelerators and Incubators

YC, Techstars, and similar programs could offer AI CTO as a resource to portfolio companies as part of their program.

### Developer Educators

Educators creating content about architecture, SaaS building, and product development can use AI CTO analyses as teaching material.

## User Journey Map

### Discovery

- User sees "SaaS Score" for a popular repo on Twitter
- User reads a post on IndieHackers about using AI CTO
- User searches "AI code review startup" or "SaaS technical audit tool"

### Activation

1. User lands on homepage
2. User connects GitHub account (OAuth)
3. User selects repository to analyze
4. Analysis completes in 60–90 seconds
5. User views their first AI CTO report
6. **Aha Moment**: User sees a specific, surprising insight they had not considered

### Retention

- User returns to check updated analysis after making changes
- User adds a second project
- User sets up weekly digest notifications
- User uses the AI Chat advisor for follow-up questions

### Expansion

- User upgrades from Free to Pro when they hit project limit
- User invites a co-founder or team member
- User upgrades to Team when collaboration becomes necessary

### Advocacy

- User tweets their SaaS Score
- User adds "Analyzed by AI CTO" badge to README
- User writes about AI CTO on IndieHackers

## What Users Are NOT Looking For

- Another linter or static analysis tool
- More dashboards and charts with no context
- Generic AI advice they could get from ChatGPT
- Slow tools that take hours to run
- Expensive tools with unclear ROI

These anti-personas help us stay focused on what makes AI CTO unique.
